Import Export Management

Introduction to Incoterms

  • Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) are a set of globally recognized rules published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC).
  • They define the responsibilities of sellers and buyers for the delivery of goods under sales contracts.
  • They specify who is responsible for paying and managing the shipment, insurance, documentation, and customs clearance.
  • The latest version is Incoterms 2020.

Why are Incoterms Important?

  • Clarity and Risk Mitigation: They prevent misunderstandings by clearly defining the point at which risk transfers from the seller to the buyer.
  • Cost Allocation: They allocate costs for shipping, insurance, and other logistics expenses.
  • Global Standardization: They provide a common language for international trade, making transactions smoother and more efficient worldwide.

Key Concepts in Incoterms

  • Delivery: The point at which the risk of loss or damage to the goods transfers from the seller to the buyer.
  • Cost: Who is responsible for paying for transport, insurance, and other costs.
  • Risk: Who bears the risk of loss or damage to the goods during transit.
  • Obligations: The duties of both parties, such as arranging for transport and providing documents.

Categories of Incoterms

Incoterms are divided into two main groups based on the mode of transport:

  • Rules for any mode or modes of transport: EXW, FCA, CPT, CIP, DAP, DPU, DDP.
  • Rules for sea and inland waterway transport: FAS, FOB, CFR, CIF.

Incoterms Explained: Group E (Departure)

EXW (Ex Works)

  • The seller makes the goods available at their own premises.
  • The buyer bears all risks and costs from that point.
  • This term places the minimum obligation on the seller.

Incoterms Explained: Group F (Main Carriage Unpaid)

  • FCA (Free Carrier): The seller delivers the goods to the carrier nominated by the buyer at a named place.
  • FAS (Free Alongside Ship): The seller delivers the goods alongside the vessel at the named port of shipment.
  • FOB (Free on Board): The seller delivers the goods on board the vessel nominated by the buyer. Risk transfers to the buyer when the goods are on board.

Incoterms Explained: Group C (Main Carriage Paid)

  • CPT (Carriage Paid To): Seller pays for carriage of goods to the named destination. Risk transfers when goods are handed to the first carrier.
  • CIP (Carriage and Insurance Paid To): Similar to CPT, but the seller must also obtain a higher level of insurance.

Incoterms Explained: Group C (continued)

  • CFR (Cost and Freight): Seller pays costs and freight to the named port of destination. Risk transfers once goods are on board the vessel.
  • CIF (Cost, Insurance and Freight): Similar to CFR, but the seller is also responsible for providing minimum insurance coverage.

Incoterms Explained: Group D (Arrival)

  • DAP (Delivered at Place): Seller delivers goods to a named destination. Buyer handles import clearance.
  • DPU (Delivered at Place Unloaded): Seller delivers to and unloads goods at the named destination.
  • DDP (Delivered Duty Paid): The seller is responsible for all costs and risks, including customs duties, taxes, and final delivery. This is the maximum seller obligation.

Choosing the Right Incoterm

  • The choice depends on:
  • Buyer/Seller Relationship
  • Level of Experience
  • Type of Goods
  • Mode of Transport

Incoterm Application: EXW Case Study

  • Scenario: A German manufacturer sells machine parts to a U.S. company and prefers not to handle export formalities.
  • Incoterm: EXW (Ex Works) is the most suitable. The U.S. buyer manages all logistics from the German factory, including export clearance, freight, and import formalities.

Incoterm Application: FOB Case Study

  • Scenario: A Brazilian exporter sells coffee beans to a Japanese importer who wants to control shipping costs.
  • Incoterm: FOB (Free on Board) is the best choice. The Brazilian seller delivers the goods on board the vessel, and the Japanese buyer takes responsibility from that point.

Incoterm Application: DDP Case Study

  • Scenario: A Chinese e-commerce company sells electronics directly to UK customers, aiming for a seamless door-to-door service with no extra charges.
  • Incoterm: DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) is the ideal term. The seller is responsible for all costs and risks, including customs duties and taxes, ensuring direct delivery to the customer's doorstep.

Incoterms and Risk Transfer

Understanding the point of risk transfer is crucial for both parties. Risk generally transfers from seller to buyer the further along the journey the terms go.

Incoterms and Cost Allocation

The Incoterm used directly determines who pays for what. Responsibilities range from minimal to maximal for the seller.

Seller's Costs

  • Export packing
  • Loading
  • Export customs clearance
  • Carriage to the point of delivery

Buyer's Costs

  • Main carriage
  • Import clearance
  • Duties, taxes, and final delivery

Incoterms and Documentation

  • Commercial Invoice: Required for customs.
  • Bill of Lading: A transport document for sea freight.
  • Packing List: Details the contents of the shipment.
  • Certificate of Origin: Proves where the goods were made.

Incoterms and Insurance

  • Insurance is a critical aspect of risk management.
  • CIP and CIF are the only Incoterms that explicitly require the seller to purchase insurance for the buyer.
  • For other terms, the party that bears the risk should arrange for insurance coverage.

Incoterms vs. Contract of Sale

  • Incoterms are not the contract of sale.
  • They only cover the specific aspects of delivery, risk, and cost.
  • The contract of sale should include other vital details such as product specifications, price, and payment terms.

Incoterms 2020: Key Changes

  • DPU replaces DAT: "Delivered at Terminal" was renamed to Delivered at Place Unloaded.
  • Insurance levels: CIP now requires a higher level of insurance (Clause A) while CIF retains the minimum level (Clause C).
  • FCA with Own Transport: The new version allows for the use of the buyer's or seller's own vehicles.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using the wrong Incoterm: Using a sea-only term (e.g., FOB) for air freight.
  • Not specifying the place: Simply stating "FOB" is insufficient.
  • Ignoring a term's implications: Not understanding the full costs of a DDP term.
  • Failure to communicate: Not clearly communicating the chosen Incoterm to all parties.

Incoterms and Global Logistics

  • Incoterms are the foundation for managing logistics in international trade.
  • They dictate:
  • Freight Forwarding: Who selects and pays the forwarder.
  • Customs Brokerage: Who is responsible for customs clearance.
  • Carrier Selection: Who contracts the shipping carrier.
